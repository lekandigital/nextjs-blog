Why the Micro-ISP Model Excites Me

The financial structure is compelling with each node requiring minimal upfront capital while generating sustainable revenue ($50-80/month or possibly lower vs Starlink's direct $120+/month). This creates a path to bootstrap community by community, proving unit economics before scaling.

More importantly, as satellite ISP costs decrease over time, differentiation will come from value-added services, and systematic data collection for Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) could provide that edge.

Geographic Focus

I'm most interested in working within US territories, particularly American Samoa as the primary target. American Samoa offers ideal conditions with severe connectivity gaps, US regulatory framework, and a manageable 55,000 population for piloting.

The Data Collection Opportunity

My core insight: expanding beyond pure ISP service to systematic data collection for PINN training. The infrastructure becomes a distributed platform for capturing human-in-the-loop data where people interact with physical systems.

For example, a farmer's irrigation timing, planting methods, or equipment usage becomes ground truth data that anchors agricultural PINNs, preventing models from converging to mathematically consistent but practically useless solutions.

The key advantage is capturing this data now from communities where high satellite startup costs ($600 equipment + $120/month) create barriers to connectivity. By providing affordable access through shared infrastructure in exchange for participating in data collection tasks, we can document human practices and decision-making that would otherwise remain invisible to researchers.

This human-anchored data is especially valuable in early PINN development when models aren't mature and need real-world calibration. The same principle applies to robotics (documenting human pick-and-place tasks) or anywhere humans constrain or guide physical systems governed by PDEs.
